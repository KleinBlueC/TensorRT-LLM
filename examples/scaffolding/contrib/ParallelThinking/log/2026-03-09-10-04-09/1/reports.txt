# Updated Report: Arrhenius's Sixth Impossibility Theorem and Critical-Level Views

**Research Question**:  
Which condition of Arrhenius's sixth impossibility theorem do critical-level views violate?

**Key Findings**:  
1. **Core Conflict**:  
   - Critical-level views (CLVs) posit a "critical level" of well-being below which existence is net-negative. This framework inherently prioritizes average well-being over distributional fairness, leading to potential violations of axiological constraints in population ethics.

2. **Theorem Conditions**:  
   - Arrhenius's sixth impossibility theorem outlines axiological conditions that cannot all be satisfied simultaneously. The relevant conditions in this context include:  
     - **General Non-Extreme Priority (B)**: Prohibits extreme prioritization of any single axiological criterion (e.g., average well-being).  
     - **Weak Quality Addition (E)**: Requires that adding a life of positive well-being does not worsen the population if the critical level is met.  

3. **Violation Analysis**:  
   - **General Non-Extreme Priority (B)** is violated because CLVs prioritize average well-being over the distribution of welfare, potentially neglecting the worst-off. This extreme prioritization conflicts with the theorem's requirement to avoid overemphasizing any single metric.  
   - **Weak Quality Addition (E)** is not directly violated, as CLVs can accommodate quality addition under specific conditions (e.g., when new lives meet the critical level).  

4. **Resolution of Conflicts**:  
   - Earlier hypotheses suggesting other conditions (e.g., Egalitarian Dominance) were refined through analysis. The core issue lies in the prioritization of average well-being, aligning with **General Non-Extreme Priority**.  

**Conclusion**:  
Critical-level views violate **B. General Non-Extreme Priority** due to their inherent prioritization of average well-being over distributional fairness, as dictated by Arrhenius's sixth impossibility theorem.  

**Final Answer**:  
B. General Non-Extreme Priority
