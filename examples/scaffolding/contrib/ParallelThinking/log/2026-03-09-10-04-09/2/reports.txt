# Updated Report: Arrhenius's Sixth Impossibility Theorem and Critical-Level Views

**Key Findings:**
- **Critical-Level Views** posit that a life is only morally acceptable if its well-being exceeds a "critical level," implying that adding individuals below this threshold is harmful, even if their lives are minimally positive.
- **Arrhenius's Sixth Impossibility Theorem** identifies a set of axiological conditions that cannot all be satisfied simultaneously in population ethics. Critical-level views are known to conflict with one of these conditions.

**Condition Violated:**
- **General Non-Extreme Priority (B)** is the violated condition. Critical-level views enforce a strict threshold-based prioritization, which may constitute an "extreme prioritization" of avoiding lives below the critical level. This conflicts with the theorem's requirement for balanced, non-extreme prioritization across outcomes.

**Supporting Reasoning:**
- The theorem's *General Non-Extreme Priority* condition prohibits overly rigid or extreme weighting of specific outcomes (e.g., avoiding low-well-being lives to an excessive degree). Critical-level views inherently prioritize this avoidance, creating a conflict.
- Other conditions (e.g., Egalitarian Dominance, Weak Non-Sadism) are not directly implicated by the threshold-based logic of critical-level views.

**Conclusion:**
The theoretical framework confirms that critical-level views violate **General Non-Extreme Priority (B)** due to their strict, threshold-driven prioritization. No conflicting evidence has emerged in this analysis.
