# Updated Report: Arrhenius’s Sixth Impossibility Theorem and Critical-Level Views  

## Key Findings  
1. **Core Question**: Identifies which condition of Arrhenius’s sixth impossibility theorem is violated by critical-level views in population ethics.  

2. **Critical-Level Views**:  
   - Posit that population value depends on whether the average welfare exceeds a fixed "critical level."  
   - If average welfare is below this level, adding more people (even with positive welfare) may be deemed worse than existing states.  

3. **Theorem Conditions**:  
   - **Non-Elitism (C)**: Requires that if two populations have the same total welfare, the one with more people (and higher average welfare) is better.  
   - **Weak Quality Addition (E)**: States that adding a person with higher quality of life should not make the population worse.  

## Analysis of Violations  
- **Non-Elitism (C)**:  
  - Critical-level views prioritize average welfare over total welfare.  
  - They may reject the preference for larger populations with higher average welfare if the average falls below the critical threshold, directly conflicting with Non-Elitism.  
  - This aligns with the theorem’s structure, as Non-Elitism emphasizes distributional fairness, which critical-level views explicitly subvert.  

- **Weak Quality Addition (E)**:  
  - Less likely to be violated, as critical-level views focus on average welfare thresholds rather than the marginal quality of added individuals.  
  - Violations would require specific scenarios where adding a high-quality individual lowers the average below the critical level, which is not a central feature of the theory.  

## Resolution of Ambiguity  
- **Prior Knowledge**: Academic literature on population ethics suggests that critical-level views are most closely associated with violating **Non-Elitism** due to their emphasis on average welfare over distributional fairness.  
- **Conflict Resolution**: While Weak Quality Addition could theoretically be affected, the evidence and logical structure of the theorem point more strongly to Non-Elitism as the violated condition.  

## Conclusion  
The most plausible answer is **C. Non-Elitism**, as critical-level views explicitly prioritize average welfare thresholds over principles of distributional fairness, directly contradicting this condition of Arrhenius’s sixth impossibility theorem.  

**Final Answer**: C. Non-Elitism
